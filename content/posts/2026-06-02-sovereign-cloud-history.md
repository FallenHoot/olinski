Sovereign clouds emerged after Snowden in 2013.

The idea: governments and large enterprises worried about surveillance wanted cloud infrastructure they could control, verify, and audit.

Fair concern.

But the execution has been expensive, complex, and largely abandoned by enterprises.

## Why this matters

History teaches why sovereign cloud models break:

1. **Cost:** Building separate cloud infrastructure is 2-3x more expensive than integrating with global clouds.
2. **Talent:** Finding local engineers to maintain sovereign clouds is hard (small market).
3. **Features:** Sovereign clouds are perpetually behind on features (certification lag).
4. **Geopolitics:** Sovereignty requirements change with politics (Brexit, trade wars, sanctions).

Result: Enterprises use sovereign clouds for compliance reasons, then workaround limitations with cheaper alternatives, nullifying the compliance benefit.

## Framework or model

What actually evolved:

- EU opted for GDPR + audit rights (not separate cloud).
- Finance adopted isolated regions with regional management (still using global cloud).
- Healthcare used compliance frameworks (not separate infrastructure).

None needed separate sovereign clouds.

## Practical perspective

Sovereign clouds work for:

- Small, isolated markets where alternatives don't exist (very rare).
- Deep government integration (health data management, government records).

Sovereign clouds fail for:

- Any enterprise that needs global scale (features, cost, talent).
- Any use case where interoperability matters.

## My take

The sovereign cloud model was a good idea in 2013 when surveillance concerns were acute and global clouds were less trustworthy.

By 2026, audit rights, encryption, and regional infrastructure make separate sovereign clouds unnecessary for most entreprises.

The model will persist in a few markets (China, Russia, maybe EU).

Everywhere else, it is legacy.
