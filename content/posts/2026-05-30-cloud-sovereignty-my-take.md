Sovereign clouds are supposed to keep data within borders.

The logic is sound: data is sensitive, governments want control.

The implementation is broken.

Most sovereign clouds are run by local partners with outdated tooling, limited regions, and higher costs.

The result: teams avoid them, use workarounds, and do not actually comply.

## Why this matters

Compliance is real.

But compliance in a sovereign cloud often requires:

- Higher cost (smaller market, local support infrastructure).
- Fewer features (partner keeps sovereign cloud intentionally limited).
- Delayed updates (partner certification lag).

Most enterprises are frustrated enough to try non-compliant workarounds.

That is worse than just not using cloud.

## Framework or model

What works:

1. **True compliance:** Data residency laws, audit rights, legal jurisdiction (UK, EU, Canada, Australia have these).
2. **Fake sovereignty:** "Sovereign" clouds that are really just regional instances with restrictions.

Picking the right one matters.

## My take

Sovereignty as a concept is good.

Executed as separate clouds, it usually fails.

Better model: integrated global cloud with regional privacy controls and audit hooks.

That is harder to market, but it works.

## What to do this week

1. If you have compliance requirements, talk to your cloud provider about audit rights and residency.
2. Do not assume a "sovereign cloud" automatically solves your compliance problem.
3. Read the fine print: where is data stored? Who can access it? How do you verify?

Compliance is hard.
Sovereign clouds are not always the answer.
